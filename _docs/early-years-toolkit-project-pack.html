The Brief

Planning, made gentle.

A growing library of beautiful, educator-made templates, built for Australian early years settings. Ready to print, plan, display, and observe. Made by someone who has actually used them in a classroom.

The problem

The biggest stress point in early years isn't behaviour or nappy changes. It's the planning. Educators spend hours every week trying to create programming that is beautiful, meaningful, and linked to the EYLF. Most do it alone, on their own time, with a blank Word doc and no system.

"One of the biggest stress points in early years isn't behaviour or nappies. It's the planning. And no one talks about it."

The solution

A subscription library of ready-to-use planning templates, designed specifically for the Australian early years context. Topic calendars, activity planners, development charts, room display labels, reflective journals, observation records, and weekly routines. New templates every month.

Beautiful enough to be proud of. Practical enough to actually use every week.

Why Clare

20 years in early years classrooms, UK to Brisbane in September 2025. Three businesses run successfully. These templates were built in the classroom, not designed from the outside in.

Who it's for

Family daycare educators and room leaders in Australian childcare centres. The educator doing the planning, every week, on their own time. And the directors who want consistency across their whole team.

What's different

EYLF v2.0 and NQF-aligned by design. Made to look beautiful on a display wall, not just functional on paper. For educators, by an educator. Parents notice the difference.

Why Existing Options Fall Short

What's out there right now

Generic templates (Etsy, Teachers Pay Teachers)

Plenty of templates exist online. The problem is they're made for the US or UK curriculum, use the wrong terminology, and look the same as everyone else's. They don't reflect EYLF v2.0 or the NQF. And if the framework changes, you're stuck with outdated files.

AI tools (Mana, ChatGPT)

AI tools like Mana are excellent at writing learning stories and observation narratives from voice notes. That's a genuinely different problem. They don't help with programming, weekly planning, room displays, or activity calendars. They're a complement to planning templates, not a replacement.

The Market

Who's out there and how to reach them

Australia's early childhood sector is large, heavily regulated, and in the middle of a workforce shortage. The demand for better planning resources is real. The question is whether this product can reach enough of that market to be a viable business.

How big is the market?

18,000 family daycare educators across Australia, plus approximately 3,000 to 5,000 educators in small and independent centres whose employers won't pay for an enterprise platform. Total addressable market: somewhere between 21,000 and 23,000 individuals.

Realistic capture at 5-10%: 1,000 to 2,000 subscribers
Source: FDCA 2024, ACECQA ECEC Snapshot

What does that mean in revenue?

1,000 subscribers at $20 per month is $20,000 per month in recurring revenue. At $25 per month, it's $25,000. That's a viable small business, but getting to 1,000 subscribers takes real, consistent effort over 12 to 24 months.

Break-even point is likely somewhere around 400 to 600 subscribers depending on costs.

Starting target: 20 paying subscribers by 1 July 2026
Things Worth Knowing

The best free distribution channels are Facebook groups. "Family Day Care Australia," "Family Day Care Educators Australia," and the state-based groups (NSW, VIC, QLD) are where this audience is most active and most likely to share resources with each other.

Instagram works well for visual content, which planning templates are. Hashtags like #eylf, #familydaycare, and #earlychildhoodaustralia get consistent engagement from the right people.

Family Day Care Australia (FDCA) represents all 18,000 educators nationally. A partnership or endorsement from them would be worth more than months of paid advertising. Worth approaching well before launch.

EYLF v1.0 came out in 2009. v2.0 came out in 2022. That's 13 years between major updates. The NQS has minor revisions every few years, but no overhaul. "Always updated when regulations change" is a fair thing to say, but in practice major changes happen roughly once a decade.

This matters for subscription retention. "Up to date with the EYLF" is a legitimate reason to subscribe, but it's not a reason to stay subscribed year after year. The real retention argument is new monthly content and a consistent system educators rely on.

The biggest risk with a template library is that someone subscribes, downloads everything in month one, then cancels. Estimated first-month churn for template-heavy services is 30 to 40%. This has to be planned against from the start.

The best way to reduce it is to release content progressively, so month one subscribers don't have access to everything at once. Seasonal content helps too, since Term 3 templates are only relevant in Term 3. Monthly new releases give people a reason to stay.

Butler Diaries ships more than 35,000 physical EYLF and NQS-aligned programming diaries every year, with educators regularly crediting them with helping achieve Exceeding ratings. This shows that digital tools haven't fully solved the planning problem. Educators trust something they can hold and write in during an assessment visit.

This is actually good news for a digital planning template product. The demand for well-designed planning resources is proven and strong. The opportunity is to offer the same quality with the flexibility of digital files.

Source: Butler Diaries website

Organic growth through Facebook groups and Instagram costs essentially nothing but takes 12 to 24 months to gain real traction. Paid Facebook ads targeting family daycare educators in Australia cost roughly $50 to $150 per subscriber acquired. A partnership with FDCA could bring that cost down to $20 to $50 per subscriber.

At a $20 per month subscription, a $100 acquisition cost means you need 6 months of retention to break even on that subscriber. Retention strategy is as important as the marketing strategy.

Options

Three ways this could go

Option A is the original idea. Start there. Options B and C are either alternative directions if validation doesn't support Option A, or things to consider running alongside it. The validation tests (next tab) will help decide.

A
Planning template library subscription
A growing library of beautiful, EYLF-aligned planning templates. New packs every month. $15 to $25 per month.
Clare's original idea

This is the core concept. A subscription library built for Australian early years educators, with new template packs released each month. Topic calendars, activity planners, weekly routines, development charts, room display labels, observation records, reflective journals. Beautiful, print-ready, and linked to EYLF v2.0.

Start lean. A simple download page and an email list. No platform needed until it's proven. The first three months are about testing whether people pay and whether they come back for month two.

Pricing range: $15 to $25 per month for individuals. $50 to $100 per month for centre team plans (3 to 5 educators). Annual pricing option to reduce churn.

What's working in this direction

  • Proven demand (Butler Diaries sells 35,000 physical diaries a year)
  • Clare's classroom experience is the genuine differentiator
  • Low cost to start, no tech required
  • Can be built progressively while earning

What needs solving

  • Aussie Childcare Network has a 15-year head start and 60,000 users
  • Download-and-cancel churn risk is high for template products
  • Monthly content production takes real time to maintain
  • Design quality has to be genuinely better to stand out
B
Assessment prep consulting
Paid engagements helping centres prepare for NQS assessment and rating visits.

Centres preparing for NQS assessment already pay $5,000 to $10,000 for consultants. Nobody currently positions specifically as an "assessment prep" specialist with the combination of planning documentation expertise and classroom experience that Clare has.

This isn't instead of Option A. It's a way to generate cash flow while Option A is being validated and built. Each consulting engagement also builds reputation and testimonials in exactly the target market.

Strengths

  • Immediate income potential
  • Builds trust and reputation in the sector
  • No platform or tech needed to start
  • Generates real insight into what centres need

Limitations

  • Trades hours for dollars, doesn't scale easily
  • Requires sales conversations and client management
  • Not a path to national reach on its own
C
Templates plus monthly live sessions
Option A's template library, plus a monthly live Q&A or planning workshop. $25 to $35 per month.

The biggest risk with a template subscription is that people download everything in month one and cancel. Adding a live monthly session, even just 30 minutes, solves that problem. You can't download a live session. It creates a reason to stay beyond the templates themselves.

This would be Option A evolved. Start with A to prove the concept, then add the live component once there's a subscriber base to justify it. A monthly "planning session" where Clare shows educators how to use the new templates and answers questions live would be genuinely different from anything a competitor currently offers.

Strengths

  • Directly addresses the download-and-cancel problem
  • Creates community and personal connection
  • Justifies a higher price point
  • Clare's classroom experience shines in a live format

Things to consider

  • More time commitment for Clare each month
  • Needs a subscriber base large enough to make live sessions worthwhile
  • Scheduling across time zones requires thought

Before You Launch

Key decisions to work through

These are the questions that need a real answer before the first template goes on sale. Click each one to see why it matters and what to do about it. They're organised roughly in the order they need to be tackled.

Research first
1
Have you actually used Aussie Childcare Network and Starskills?
▼

Not browsed their website. Actually subscribed, downloaded their templates, and spent time with their products. The whole competitive argument depends on understanding where they fall short. There's no shortcut to this.

Action: Subscribe to both for one month (~$50 total). Document every template they offer. Note the gaps. This is the most important research step before building anything.
2
Have you spoken to at least ten family daycare educators about their planning?
▼

Not pitched to. Actually listened to. The goal is to hear them describe their planning week in their own words, find out what they currently use, and understand what a better solution looks like to them. The templates have to solve their actual problem, not the assumed version of it.

Action: Find educators through FDC Facebook groups or your existing network. Have a genuine conversation. Don't mention what you're building until after you've heard them out.
Product design
3
What does the first template pack actually contain?
▼

The launch pack needs to feel complete, not like a teaser. Educators should be able to open it and plan an entire week immediately. What's included, what format (PDF, Canva, Word), what age groups, and what EYLF outcomes does it cover?

Recommendation: Aim for a pack that covers a full week: weekly plan, 3 to 4 activity planners, an observation page, and a display label set. Canva editable so educators can personalise it.
4
How do you stop people downloading everything and cancelling?
▼

Template subscriptions have a real churn problem. Someone subscribes for $20, downloads the whole library, and cancels before month two. This has to be thought through before opening subscriptions.

Options: Release content progressively so older subscribers access more. Use seasonal packs (Term 3 templates only land in Term 3). Add a monthly live planning session that can't be downloaded. Annual pricing reduces impulse cancellations.
5
Is every template checked against EYLF v2.0?
▼

EYLF v2.0 came out in 2022 and updated some language and outcomes. Any template referencing the old framework language is out of date. If an educator spots outdated outcomes during an assessment visit, it reflects badly on the product and on them.

Action: Before publishing anything, check every outcome reference against the current ACECQA EYLF v2.0 document. Keep a checklist. Review again whenever the NQS is updated.
Business setup
6
Is the business registered in Australia?
▼

An ABN, a business bank account, and GST registration (required once turnover passes $75,000 per year) all need to be in place before taking any payments. It takes a few hours and costs under $100.

Action: Register ABN through abr.gov.au. Register business name through asic.gov.au (~$39 per year). Open a separate business bank account. Done.
7
Are the subscriber terms sorted before anyone pays?
▼

Australian consumer law has specific requirements around subscriptions, including cancellation rights and refund policies. Terms and conditions and a privacy policy both need to be in place before the first payment is taken. This isn't optional.

Recommendation: Use a subscription terms template as a starting point and have it reviewed by a local lawyer. Budget $300 to $500 for this. It's a one-time cost that protects the business long term.
Reaching people
8
Is there an email list being built right now?
▼

The strongest launch is to a warm audience. An email list of 500 people who already know what the product is means day one isn't starting from zero. Every month before launch is time to build that list.

Action: Set up a free Mailchimp or Kit account. Add a simple landing page with an email signup. Start posting useful planning content in FDC Facebook groups to build awareness. Collect emails before the product is ready.
9
Has anyone talked to Family Day Care Australia?
▼

FDCA represents all 18,000 family daycare educators nationally. A partnership or endorsement from them would reach the entire target market in one step. That's worth more than months of paid advertising.

Action: Send FDCA a short introduction email. Explain what the product is and propose a member discount arrangement. Start this conversation well before launch, not on launch day.
